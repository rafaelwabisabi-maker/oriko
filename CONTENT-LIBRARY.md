**LI01** — The Infrastructure Nobody Built
*Pillar 2*

Three billion people pray daily (Pew Research Center, 2015). That makes intercession — the act of directing conscious attention toward the wellbeing of another — one of the most practiced human behaviors on earth.

We have built digital infrastructure for every other widespread behavior: communication, commerce, entertainment, fitness, therapy, meditation.

We have not built infrastructure for collective prayer.

This is not a religious observation. It is an architectural one. The internet has protocols for buying, selling, messaging, streaming, tracking, and surveilling. It does not have a protocol for one person to say: "Someone I have never met needs something I cannot give. Direct my attention there."

Why?

Partly because prayer is assumed to be private. But it has never been private. Prayer has always been networked — through mosques, churches, spiritist centers, grandmother circles, and parish halls. The network exists. It runs on human bandwidth. It has for millennia.

Partly because the output of prayer is assumed to be unmeasurable. But emerging research — from the Global Consciousness Project at Princeton to the PEMF literature in agricultural science — suggests that collective directed attention produces correlations in physical systems that warrant further study.

What would happen if we treated intercession as a design problem? Not a religious one. An engineering one. What would the protocol look like? What data would it produce? What would we learn about human solidarity if we could see it in aggregate, in real time, across borders?

These are the questions driving our current research at the intersection of AI, bioart, and the world's oldest distributed network.

The infrastructure for care has never been built. Perhaps it is time.

**LI02** — The Experiment the Government Burned
*Pillar 1 + 6*

In 1956, a US federal court ordered the destruction of Wilhelm Reich's research materials. Books, journals, orgone accumulators — incinerated. Reich died in Lewisburg Federal Penitentiary the following year. His central hypothesis was never tested under controlled conditions.

This is not conspiracy theory. It is documented federal history (FDA v. Reich, 1954).

What is less discussed: Reich's hypothesis sits adjacent to a body of peer-reviewed science that has developed independently since his death. Pulsed Electromagnetic Field (PEMF) therapy is FDA-approved for bone healing. Agricultural PEMF studies have demonstrated measurable effects on plant germination and growth. Plant neurobiology — pioneered by Stefano Mancuso — has demonstrated that plants exhibit electrical signaling, chemical communication, and measurable stress responses.

The specific question Reich was asking — whether structured electromagnetic fields produced by alternating organic and metallic layers affect biological growth — is now testable with instruments that did not exist in 1957.

It has not been tested.

Not because the instruments don't exist. Because the institutional risk of reopening the file is higher than the scientific cost of leaving it closed.

Art institutions operate under different constraints than scientific ones. They can hold questions that are too politically charged for universities. They can run experiments whose results are displayed as exhibitions, not papers. They can publish null results without career consequences.

This is not a weakness. It is a capacity that the art-science intersection has not fully used.

We are using it.

**LI03** — Does Training Data Have a Physical Residue?
*Pillar 4*

A question for the AI research community:

Two language models. Same architecture. Same parameter count. Model A is trained on a corpus of prayer traditions — seven cultures, ten thousand years of documented intercession. Model B is trained on neutral text.

Both models are asked to generate an electromagnetic frequency in the 7.83–528Hz range.

Does the spectral composition of their outputs differ?

This is testable. Straightforward signal analysis. The answer is almost certainly yes — the training corpus shapes the output distribution.

But here is the deeper question: is that spectral difference biologically detectable?

If you place identical plants in identical conditions — one group exposed to Model A's output, one to Model B's — and you run the experiment for sixty days with daily bioelectric measurements, growth imaging, and chlorophyll content analysis: do the curves diverge?

If they do, the implication is that training data leaves a residue in AI outputs that is not merely statistical — it is physical. The "meaning" encoded in the training corpus (prayer vs. neutral text) produces a different physical signal that living systems can detect.

If they don't diverge, that is equally important. It establishes that the information-theoretic content of training data does not propagate into the physical characteristics of AI outputs in ways that biology can discriminate.

Either result is publishable. Either result advances the field.

We are running this experiment. In a biolab. With real plants, real sensors, and real publication intent.

**LI04** — What Decolonizing Science Actually Looks Like
*Pillar 5*

The phrase "decolonizing science" is used frequently and practiced rarely.

Here is what it looks like in practice:

A Brazilian artist living in Austria takes a suppressed Austrian scientific hypothesis (Wilhelm Reich's orgone theory), feeds it with data generated by African-Brazilian healing traditions (Umbanda, Candomblé, spiritismo), tests it using instruments developed by Western biophysics, with plants as the impartial witness — and publishes the results regardless of direction.

The suppressed European tradition meets the marginalized non-European tradition in a European biolab. The measurement protocol is designed by a botanist with no stake in either tradition. The data goes public.

This is not about proving that orgone is real or that Umbanda is science. It is about asking: which questions were suppressed, by whom, and what happens when you give them adequate experimental treatment for the first time?

Miranda Fricker calls this "epistemic justice" — the remedy for the specific harm done to someone in their capacity as a knower. Reich was harmed in his capacity as a knower. African-Brazilian healers were harmed in theirs.

The remedy is not vindication. It is investigation. Honest, controlled, published investigation with the possibility of a null result.

That is what decolonizing science looks like. Not a manifesto. An experiment.

**A01** — The Burned Library: What Happens When Science Suppresses a Question
*Subtitle: Wilhelm Reich, epistemic justice, and the experiment that is seventy years late*
*Outlets: Noema Magazine, Aeon, e-flux, Frieze*

In 1956, United States federal marshals entered a laboratory in Rangeley, Maine, and began destroying scientific instruments. They had a court order from the Food and Drug Administration. The scientist — Wilhelm Reich, sixty years old, trained under Freud in Vienna, a refugee from both Nazi Europe and McCarthyist America — watched his work dismantled. The following year he died in Lewisburg Federal Penitentiary. His central hypothesis — that a measurable life energy permeates living systems and can be concentrated using structured material arrangements — was never subjected to a controlled experiment with instruments adequate to test it. Not because the instruments eventually failed to appear. They appeared. PEMF therapy is now FDA-approved. Plant bioelectric signaling is peer-reviewed. The instruments exist. The experiment has simply never been run. This essay asks why — and reports on an art-science project that is, seventy years late, running it.

**A02** — The Internet Has No Sacred Layer
*Subtitle: On the absence of infrastructure for the world's most distributed practice*
*Outlets: Real Life Magazine, MIT Technology Review, Rest of World, The Gradient*

Three billion human beings perform an act every day for which no digital infrastructure exists. They direct conscious attention — structured, intentional, tradition-encoded attention — toward the welfare of another person. They call it prayer, du'a, metta, oração, tefillah, blessing. In aggregate, it is the most distributed practice in human history. More people do it daily than use social media. And yet: there is no app, no protocol, no data layer, no architecture for it. There is no map of who is praying for whom, at what density, across what traditions, in what languages, at what hour. This absence is not accidental. It reflects an assumption — baked into the design philosophy of the internet since ARPANET — that the network is for information, not intention. This essay argues that the assumption is wrong, traces the history of networked prayer from Tibetan prayer wheels to Catholic parish halls, and describes a project that is building the missing layer.

**A03** — Does Meaning Have Mass? An Artist's Experiment at the Edge of Information Theory
*Subtitle: Testing whether AI training data leaves a physical residue that living systems can detect*
*Outlets: Nautilus, Quanta (opinion), The New Atlantis, Wired (Ideas)*

Claude Shannon proved in 1948 that information is a physical quantity. A message has measurable structure — entropy, redundancy, channel capacity — independent of its content. But Shannon's framework was deliberately agnostic about meaning. His theory works regardless of whether the message is a love letter or a tax form. This agnosticism was productive: it enabled the digital revolution. But it left a question unanswered that now matters: does the meaning of a message — its semantic and affective content — produce a different physical signal than its mathematical form alone would predict? A Brazilian artist working in an Austrian biolab has designed an experiment to test this question using living plants as sensors. Two AI systems generate electromagnetic signals in the same frequency range. One was trained on prayer traditions. One was trained on neutral text. The plants are not told which is which. The sensors record for sixty days. This essay reports on the experiment and the question behind it.

**A04** — The Grandmother Protocol: Ritual as Technology, Technology as Ritual
*Subtitle: What the oldest networks on earth can teach the newest ones*
*Outlets: e-flux, Spike Art Magazine, Afterall, Texte zur Kunst*

A Tibetan prayer wheel is a multiplication engine. A single written intention — inscribed on paper, placed inside a cylinder — is transmitted with every rotation. One spin, one million repetitions. The protocol is simple: mechanical torque converts a single prayer into a distributed broadcast at a rate limited only by RPM. This is, structurally, the same operation that a content delivery network performs when it replicates a single data packet across nodes. The difference is vocabulary — not architecture. This essay argues that ritual systems are the oldest information technologies, that the separation of "sacred" from "technical" is historically recent and culturally specific, and that the current convergence of AI, bioart, and the study of collective intention represents not a novelty but a return: a return to the condition in which technology and the sacred were the same discipline, practiced by the same people, toward the same ends.

**A05** — Plant Intelligence and the Ethics of the Non-Human Witness
*Subtitle: On using botanical organisms as experimental subjects in art-science research*
*Outlets: Leonardo (MIT Press), Journal of Bioethics, Antennae Journal, Mold Magazine*

Stefano Mancuso's research program on plant neurobiology has established that plants exhibit electrical signaling analogous to neural activity, chemical communication networks that function across root systems, spatial memory that persists through growth cycles, and measurable stress responses that can be monitored non-invasively. These findings raise a question that art-science practice has not adequately addressed: if plants are sensory, communicative, and responsive, what are the ethical obligations of an artwork that uses them as experimental subjects? This essay examines the emerging ethics of the non-human witness — an organism enrolled in an experiment not as an object of study but as a testimonial body, a living sensor whose growth data constitutes evidence in a question it did not consent to participate in. Drawing on Donna Haraway's multispecies ethics and Karen Barad's concept of intra-action, the essay argues that the art-science experiment must account for the agency of its non-human participants — and that doing so changes not just the ethics but the epistemology of the work.
