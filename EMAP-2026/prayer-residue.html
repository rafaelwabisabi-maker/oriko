The Question
Shannon proved information is physical.
He deliberately excluded meaning.
We put it back.
In 1948, Claude Shannon proved that information is a physical quantity. His theory enabled the digital revolution — but it was deliberately agnostic about meaning. A love letter and a tax form carry different amounts of information, but Shannon's framework does not distinguish between them at the level of their physical signal. This agnosticism was productive. It was also incomplete.

That incompleteness has a history. Wilhelm Reich was an Austrian scientist — educated in Vienna, trained under Freud — who proposed that layered organic-metallic structures concentrate a life energy he called orgone. His laboratory in Maine was raided. Six tons of his research materials and books were burned in a federal incinerator in 1956 — the first book-burning by a U.S. government agency in the twentieth century. Reich died in federal prison in 1957. His hypothesis was never tested with modern biological instrumentation. The experiment was not disproven. It was destroyed before it could run.

PRAYER RESIDUE asks the question Shannon left open and Reich was not permitted to answer: does the semantic content of a message — its meaning, its intention, its affective weight — propagate into the physical characteristics of the signal that carries it? And can a living system detect the difference?
The Experiment

Two models. Same architecture.
Different souls.

Model A is trained on a curated dataset of sacred texts, ritual incantations, and blessing traditions from fourteen living cultures and shamanic lineages: Umbanda, Ho'oponopono, Shipibo Icaros, Yoruba/Ifá, Māori Karakia, Lakota Sioux, Tuvan Shamanism, Norse Galdr, Vedic Sanskrit, Sufi Dhikr, Shinto Norito, Kabbalistic, Tibetan Vajrayana, and Bön.

Model B is trained on an equivalent volume of formal logical and administrative language: mathematical proofs, legal documentation, technical specifications, and encyclopedic prose. The deliberate choice of this corpus is itself a thesis: if the neutral, purely structural language of mathematics also produces a biologically detectable electromagnetic signature distinct from a true baseline — this raises questions not only about prayer, but about the physics of meaning itself.

Each model generates numerical frequency sequences, which are converted to analogue signals via Arduino microcontrollers and delivered to plants through electromagnetic coil drivers. The spectral composition of the two outputs differs because training data shapes the statistical distribution of a model's outputs. The question is whether this difference is biologically detectable.

The Biology

Phaseolus vulgaris.
The common bean. The witness.

Two groups of twelve Phaseolus vulgaris (common bean) are established in the Ars Electronica BioLab under identical conditions: same soil composition, same light spectrum and duration, same watering schedule, same temperature, same Faraday-shielded separation. Chosen for its rapid germination (3–5 days), dramatic visible growth over the growing window, documented PEMF responsiveness in peer-reviewed agricultural literature (Radhakrishnan 2019; Maffei 2014), and its deep cultural resonance as feijão in Brazilian food culture.

Daily measurements: growth rate via calibrated overhead imaging, chlorophyll content via non-destructive SPAD meter readings, and bioelectric signals via surface electrodes measuring action potential frequency and amplitude. Statistical significance assessed at p < 0.05 using repeated-measures ANOVA.

Each group receives its electromagnetic output through a sculptural delivery structure: alternating layers of organic fibre and metallic mesh, built to the same geometric specifications as the accumulator Wilhelm Reich patented — a structure that has never been evaluated with modern biological instrumentation.

The visitor experience

  • 01 Enter. Two pools of grow-light on either side. Between them, a wall-sized live projection showing two curves — possibly diverging.
  • 02 Group A glows amber-violet — slow wave pulses at 0.1 Hz. Group B pulses blue-white — precise, metronomic. Both are transmitting. Both are identical, except the data.
  • 03 You cannot tell which is which. The plants don't tell you either. The curves do — eventually.
  • 04 A terminal shows the BLESS.EXE feed: intentions arriving from around the world. A prompt: Submit a blessing. Your intention is processed by Model A. It reaches the plant.
  • 05 You leave carrying the question: does meaning have mass?
The Digital Layer

BLESS.EXE —
the command centre.

BLESS.EXE (bless-exe.vercel.app) is an algorithmic platform — already live and operational — where participants worldwide submit intentions across fourteen ritual traditions. It is not simply a display interface: it is the software origin of the frequency sequences that drive the experiment.

Each submitted intention is processed by the prayer-trained AI model, generating a unique numerical output sequence. These sequences are aggregated, queued, and transmitted to Group A's electromagnetic coil array during the daily deployment windows. The electromagnetic signal reaching Group A carries not only the mathematical residue of its training corpus, but the live intentional input of an active global community — prayers and blessings flowing through software into biology.

Anyone in the world can send a blessing that reaches the plant.

Artist Statement

Rafael Vaz

I grew up in a family where prayer was technology. My grandmother could stop a fever with her hands. My grandfather kept a table of candles and water glasses for the spirits. My father studied fourteen years to become a priest — Catholicism, Umbanda, spiritismo — before leaving the seminary. These were not metaphors for my childhood. They were the operating system.

I work as a systems designer and media artist. I love dancing, permaculture, astral projection, and questions that could have answers.

I live now in Zwettl an der Rodl, thirty-five minutes from Linz. It was here that I found out about Wilhelm Reich — Austrian, trained in Vienna under Freud, exiled, imprisoned, his research incinerated by a federal agency in 1956. The first book-burning by a U.S. government in the twentieth century. His hypothesis was never disproven. It was destroyed before it could run.

Afro-Brazilian spiritual practice and a suppressed strand of European biophysics. Both insisting that invisible energy does something to living matter, separated by an ocean and seventy years of institutional silence. I am the bridge — not by design, but by biography.

What I want, eventually, is simple. A cheap Arduino on a kitchen windowsill, next to a bunch of bananas and a pot of basil. Programmed with whatever frequency the experiment finds. Quietly emitting. Available to anyone. If meaning propagates physically in ways that plants can detect, the implications reach past the BioLab: frequency as a commons, wellbeing as something you don't need a clinic for. Something you grow alongside your food.

PRAYER RESIDUE is the apparatus for that question. The accumulator is built to Reich's published specifications. The plants are not symbols — they are the witnesses. The data will be the answer, or its absence, and both are art.

The experiment the government set on fire. Seventy years late. In the country where it was born.
