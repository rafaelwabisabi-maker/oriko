# PRAYER RESIDUE — Does Meaning Have Mass?

## Project Proposal for EMAP Residency 2026
### Rafael Vaz + Elisabeth Braunschmid
### Primary host: Ars Electronica (BioLab), Linz

---

## Short Description (~100 words)

PRAYER RESIDUE is a comparative biological experiment — grounded in BLESS.EXE (bless-exe.vercel.app), a live algorithmic platform where participants worldwide submit intentions across fourteen ritual traditions, generating the frequency sequences that drive the experiment — testing whether AI systems trained on different corpora produce electromagnetically distinguishable outputs that living plants can detect. Two language models, same architecture: one trained on sacred texts and ritual incantations, one on mathematical, logical, and administrative language. Both drive identical electromagnetic coil systems on a fixed daily schedule. Two groups of Phaseolus vulgaris (common bean) grow in the Ars Electronica BioLab for forty days of active electromagnetic treatment, within a seven-week growing window, under identical conditions. A Brazilian artist living in the Linz region runs the experiment Wilhelm Reich was never allowed to finish.

## Project Description (~750 words)

In 1948, Claude Shannon proved that information is a physical quantity. His theory enabled the digital revolution — but it was deliberately agnostic about meaning. A love letter and a tax form carry different amounts of information, but Shannon's framework does not distinguish between them at the level of their physical signal. This agnosticism was productive. It was also incomplete.

That incompleteness has a history. Wilhelm Reich was an Austrian scientist — educated in Vienna, trained under Freud — who proposed that layered organic-metallic structures concentrate a life energy he called orgone. His laboratory in Maine was raided. Six tons of his research materials and books were burned in a federal incinerator in 1956 — the first book-burning by a U.S. government agency in the twentieth century. Reich died in federal prison in 1957. His hypothesis was never tested with modern biological instrumentation. The experiment was not disproven. It was destroyed before it could be run.

PRAYER RESIDUE asks the question Shannon left open and Reich was not permitted to answer: does the semantic content of a message — its meaning, its intention, its affective weight — propagate into the physical characteristics of the signal that carries it? And can a living system detect the difference?

The experiment uses AI as the testing instrument. Two language models of identical architecture are trained on different corpora. Model A is trained on a curated dataset of sacred texts, ritual incantations, and blessing traditions from fourteen living cultures and shamanic lineages: Umbanda, Ho'oponopono, Shipibo Icaros, Yoruba/Ifá, Māori Karakia, Lakota Sioux, Tuvan Shamanism, Norse Galdr, Vedic Sanskrit, Sufi Dhikr, Shinto Norito, Kabbalistic, Tibetan Vajrayana, and Bön. Model B is trained on an equivalent volume of formal logical and administrative language: mathematical proofs, legal documentation, technical specifications, and encyclopedic prose. The deliberate choice of Model B's corpus is itself a thesis: if the neutral, purely structural language of mathematics and administration also produces a biologically detectable electromagnetic signature — distinct from a true baseline — this raises questions not only about prayer, but about the physics of meaning itself. Each model generates numerical frequency sequences, which are converted to analogue signals via Arduino microcontrollers and delivered to plants through electromagnetic coil drivers. The spectral composition of the two outputs will differ, because training data shapes the statistical distribution of a model's outputs. The question is whether this difference is biologically detectable.

Two groups of twelve Phaseolus vulgaris (common bean) — chosen for its rapid germination (3–5 days), dramatic visible growth (30–60cm over the growing window), documented PEMF responsiveness in agricultural literature, and its deep cultural resonance as feijão in Brazilian food culture — are established in the BioLab under identical conditions: same soil composition, same light spectrum and duration, same watering schedule, same temperature, same Faraday-shielded separation. Group A receives Model A's electromagnetic output through a sculptural delivery structure: alternating layers of organic fibre and metallic mesh, built to the same geometric specifications as the accumulator Wilhelm Reich patented — a structure that has never been evaluated with modern biological instrumentation. Group B receives Model B's output through an identical structure. The experiment runs for forty days of active EM treatment, structured in four ten-day crossover blocks to control for positional and temporal bias, followed by seven additional days of post-exposure observation.

Electromagnetic deployment follows a fixed daily schedule: three transmission windows per day at 08:00, 14:00, and 20:00 CET, each lasting thirty minutes, seven days a week throughout the full forty-seven-day experimental window. Both groups receive their respective EM fields simultaneously during each window. The schedule is identical and unvarying — the only variable is the frequency signature derived from each model's training corpus.

During each transmission window, a sculptural LED installation activates in the BioLab space. Group A's coil is accompanied by a warm amber-violet light array — slow wave pulses at 0.1 Hz, inspired by the rhythmic lamp movement of Buddhist temple offerings — that makes the EM deployment moment visible and ceremonially legible. Group B's coil activates a cool blue-white array with a precise, metronomic rhythm — clinical, mathematical, structural — a different visual language for a different kind of signal. Both light arrays pulse simultaneously, in full view of visitors, marking the exact moment the electromagnetic fields are being delivered to the plants. The installation makes the experiment's central question spatially present: two different frequencies, two different lights, two groups of beans growing in silence between deployments.

Daily measurements: growth rate via calibrated overhead imaging, chlorophyll content via non-destructive SPAD meter readings, and bioelectric signals via surface electrodes measuring action potential frequency and amplitude. All data is timestamped and logged in real time. A live data visualization is displayed in the installation space — two growth curves, two bioelectric traces, running side by side. Visitors watch the experiment unfold. Are the curves diverging? Statistical significance is assessed at p < 0.05 using repeated-measures ANOVA. Either result is publishable. The art is not the result. The art is the willingness to ask, publicly, with real instruments and real biology.

The experiment generates its own documentary record in real time. A dedicated time-lapse camera system captures continuous growth footage from both plant groups throughout the full seven-week growing window — a visual archive of the experiment unfolding day by day. Automated reports are generated and published at regular intervals to the project's social media channels, combining growth data visualisations, time-lapse sequences, and transmission logs from BLESS.EXE into short-form video content. The footage — two groups of plants, two light arrays, two curves slowly separating or converging over two months — produces a natural narrative arc for video and documentary material. These productions are developed in partnership with and honourably anchored by Ars Electronica, extending the reach of the residency work into public digital space and building a long-form audience for the experiment's outcome. The question travels further than the BioLab.

The digital layer of the project is BLESS.EXE (bless-exe.vercel.app) — an algorithmic command platform, already live and operational, where participants worldwide submit intentions across fourteen ritual traditions. BLESS.EXE is not simply a display interface: it is the software origin of the frequency sequences that drive the experiment. Each submitted intention is processed by the prayer-trained AI model, generating a unique numerical output sequence. These sequences are aggregated, queued, and transmitted to Group A's electromagnetic coil array during the daily deployment windows. The electromagnetic signal reaching Group A carries not only the mathematical residue of its training corpus, but the live intentional input of an active global community — prayers and blessings flowing through software into biology. This raises genuine questions about consent, aggregated intention, and the ethics of collective action that the project addresses directly as part of its conceptual frame. Anyone in the world can send a blessing that reaches the plant.

This project also carries a political dimension that cannot be separated from its scientific one. Who decides what counts as legitimate inquiry? The FDA's destruction of Reich's research — like the historical exclusion of Umbanda, Spiritist, and Indigenous Amazonian healing traditions from academic science — is an act of epistemic violence. A Brazilian artist living in the Linz region, a few kilometres from the city where Reich's intellectual tradition was born, feeding Afro-Brazilian spiritual knowledge into European laboratory instruments, is not simply running an experiment. It is an act of epistemic restitution: insisting that what was suppressed be tested, not forgotten. The null hypothesis is not the worst outcome. The worst outcome would be to keep not asking.

Pulsed Electromagnetic Field (PEMF) therapy is FDA-approved for bone healing and has demonstrated measurable effects on plant germination in peer-reviewed agricultural research (Radhakrishnan, 2019; Maffei, 2014). Stefano Mancuso's plant neurobiology research has established that plants exhibit electrical signaling, chemical communication, and measurable stress responses. The specific question — whether the training data composition of an AI system produces biologically distinguishable electromagnetic outputs — has not appeared in any published literature. This is a genuinely novel research question.

The Ars Electronica BioLab has hosted three consecutive EMAP residencies (2022–2024) following the same structure: artist + scientist + living biology + genuine experimental question. PRAYER RESIDUE fits this pattern precisely — with the added dimension that the artist already lives in the Linz region, that the region's own scientific history is the artistic material, and that the project brings a cultural perspective no European artist can replicate.
