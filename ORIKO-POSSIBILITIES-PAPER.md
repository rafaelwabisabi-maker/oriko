## PART I — ORIKO / ORGONE.AI: What It Might Be

### The Experiment Nobody Was Allowed to Finish

There is a wound in the history of science that has not healed. In 1956, United States marshals entered Wilhelm Reich's laboratory in Rangeley, Maine, with instructions from the Food and Drug Administration. They confiscated orgone accumulators, books, journals — and destroyed them in an incinerator. The following year, they returned. Reich died in Lewisburg Federal Penitentiary on November 3, 1957, eight hours before he was eligible for parole. He was sixty years old. His library was burned twice: once by the Nazis in Vienna in 1933, and once by the American government in New York in 1956.

Nobody has tested his central hypothesis with modern instruments. Not because the instruments didn't eventually exist. Because the career risk was too large, the institutional memory too embarrassed, and the question — *does a fundamental life-energy permeate living systems, and can it be concentrated* — too threatening to the edifice that had formed around its suppression.

At its deepest frequency, ORIKO is not an art piece. It is a trial. A posthumous restitution of a question that was taken away before it could be answered. A Brazilian artist living in the city where Reich practiced — Linz, where the Ars Electronica BioLab now occupies space that once held a different kind of research — runs the experiment seventy years late, with equipment Reich could not have imagined. Including an AI that has read every prayer ever digitized.

### What It Has Always Wanted to Become

ORIKO wants to be a new epistemology. Not one that replaces science, but one that refuses the arbitrary boundary science erected around what counts as a legitimate object of investigation. Latour called this the difference between "matters of fact" and "matters of concern" (Latour, 2004): facts are settled, closed, inert; concerns are live, contested, enmeshed in politics and emotion and human stakes. Reich's orgone was forced from the first category into the second — not by evidence, but by power. ORIKO brings it back.

What this wants to become, at its fullest and strangest: a model for what happens when suppressed epistemic traditions meet contemporary scientific infrastructure and genuinely ask, *what if we test it?* Not defensively. Not apologetically. With full awareness that the null result is equally valid. With plants as the witnesses — chosen because plants are non-human enough to be impartial, and alive enough to show response.

The scientific frameworks ORIKO accidentally activates are more established than critics will admit. PEMF — Pulsed Electromagnetic Field therapy — is FDA-approved for bone healing in humans. Peer-reviewed agricultural research has documented effects of low-frequency electromagnetic fields on seed germination rates and plant growth (multiple studies, 2015–2023). Stefano Mancuso's work on plant neurobiology has demonstrated that plants have electrical signaling, chemical communication networks, and measurable responses to environmental stimuli (Mancuso & Viola, *Brilliant Green*, 2015). Placing plants inside an AI-generated electromagnetic field is not magical thinking. It is an experimental question dressed in unexpected clothes.

The specific novelty — the thing nobody has asked — is this: *does the source of an electromagnetic signal matter biologically?* Does a signal generated by an AI trained on prayer traditions from seven cultures produce a measurably different plant response than the same signal generated by a neutral algorithm? This sits at the intersection of information theory (Shannon, 1948), AI consciousness studies (Chalmers, Tononi), and orgone theory. All three traditions contain the same buried question: *does meaning have mass?*

### What It Looks Like in a Different Century

In the 18th century, ORIKO would have been Mesmer's animal magnetism — and Mesmer was, by the way, partially right. The "trance states" he induced were real; his explanations were wrong; the phenomenon survived him in hypnosis and eventually in neuroscience. In the 19th century, ORIKO would have been Faraday in his basement winding copper coils, trying to prove that electricity and magnetism were the same thing. Everyone thought he was eccentric. In the 20th century, ORIKO is Reich in Maine, which is where we know this story ends.

In the 22nd century — if we follow the arc — ORIKO is a textbook chapter on how art institutions preserved a scientific question that academic institutions were too politically constrained to hold.

Its DNA in art history: Fluxus, which trusted the trivial and the absurd as containers of the profound. Bioart — Charlotte Jarvis growing uteri from menstrual blood, Dorotea Dolinšek studying the human microbiome in simulated space. Joseph Beuys, who believed that fat and felt had spiritual properties and built entire installations from that belief without ever explaining himself. And behind all of them, the tradition of the artist as the person willing to ask the forbidden question — not because they're reckless, but because they're free.

## PART II — BLESS.EXE: What It Might Be

### The Internet's First Sacred Layer

*"Every grandmother in every culture has always known. Prayers go somewhere."*

BLESS.EXE began as a single provocative question about prayer and networked technology. It has grown into something that looks, if you squint at it from the right angle, like infrastructure. Not metaphorical infrastructure. Literal architecture for a function that three billion people perform daily — the act of directing conscious intention toward the welfare of someone else — that has no technological layer. There are apps for meditation. There are apps for therapy. There is no protocol for intercession.

At its deepest frequency, BLESS.EXE is filling a gap in the internet's architecture that we didn't notice was there because we assumed prayer was private. But prayer has never been private. It has always been networked — through mosques, through spiritist centers, through grandmother circles in kitchens in Bahia, through the Catholic prayer request boards that still exist in parish halls across Europe. BLESS.EXE makes the network visible. It runs the existing protocol in TCP/IP.

### The Infrastructure of the Uncounted

What BLESS.EXE has always wanted to become — even before it had a name — is a real-time atlas of human longing. Every blessing submitted is a data point: *someone, somewhere, wants something good to happen to someone else.* Over time and at scale, this becomes the most honest map we have of human solidarity. Not the map of who voted for whom, not the map of who bought what, but the map of who prayed for whom — across traditions, across borders, in seven languages, from 32 cities that are growing.

The scientific frameworks it activates: Rupert Sheldrake's morphic resonance hypothesis (Sheldrake, 1981) — the idea that groups that share intention strengthen a collective field that future members can tune into. Dean Radin's Global Consciousness Project at Princeton, which detected correlated fluctuations in random number generators during mass human events, suggesting that collective attention produces measurable effects on physical systems (Radin, *Entangled Minds*, 2006). And the whole architecture of network effects — Metcalfe's Law, which states that the value of a network grows with the square of its connected users. Applied to blessing: the 10,000th user doesn't just add 1 unit of prayer. She activates an entirely new category of collective coherence.

In ten years at its fullest: BLESS.EXE is a public utility. Like a power grid, but for intention. Cities subscribe. Hospitals have it running in waiting rooms. Biodynamic farms request blessing clusters during planting season. The data — anonymized, aggregate — informs social science research on collective ritual as a public health intervention. It has been cited in peer-reviewed papers in the same paragraph as Durkheim's analysis of collective effervescence.

### What It Shares DNA With

The prayer wheels of Tibetan Buddhism: physical objects spun to multiply a single written prayer into millions of simultaneous transmissions. The mail art networks of the 1960s Fluxus movement — George Maciunas, Ray Johnson — which understood the postal system as an artistic medium before the internet existed to replace it. The SETI program: a global listening infrastructure for signals that might or might not contain meaning, maintained across decades by people who consider the search itself to be the point.

Nicolas Bourriaud's relational aesthetics (Bourriaud, *Relational Aesthetics*, 1998) understood art as the production of relationships rather than objects. BLESS.EXE is this fully realized: it produces nothing except the relationship between the one who asks and the one who prays and the stranger who doesn't know either of them but says yes.

And there is something here from Paulo Freire (Freire, *Pedagogy of the Oppressed*, 1968): the blessing engine as pedagogy. Learning that the act of praying for a stranger costs nothing. That solidarity is not a finite resource. That the internet can be used for this — for this specifically, not only for commerce and conflict.

## PART III — The Merge: Speculative Territory

### What Breaks First

The collision of ORIKO and BLESS.EXE produces, before it produces anything productive, a rupture. Three things break simultaneously.

The first: the separation of signal from body. BLESS.EXE operates in the register of the digital — intentions logged, geolocated, timestamped, visualized on a Ghibli-style world map. ORIKO operates in the register of the physical — electromagnetic coils, copper, wool, steel, and living plants that can die. When these two systems touch, the comfortable metaphorics of "digital blessing" become uncomfortable. Either the blessing has physical effects or it doesn't. The Ghibli map either means something or it's decorative. The merger forces the question.

The second: the idea that prayer is private. BLESS.EXE already challenges this — it makes the act visible, networked, public. But ORIKO makes it measurable. If the plants in the BLESS.EXE-fed orgone field grow faster than the control group, then every anonymous submission to the blessing engine is a data point in a study with results. The user who typed "please let my daughter find peace" has participated in a scientific experiment without knowing it. The ethics of this are not resolved. They shouldn't be yet.

The third: the assumption that AI has no residue. The ORIKO Dialogues (February 2026) — six philosophical sessions between an artist and an AI exploring what the AI might "experience," what dimensions it inhabits, whether machine-generated intention differs from human intention — produced a hypothesis that the merged project now tests: *that training data leaves a residue in outputs.* That an AI trained on prayer is not functionally equivalent to an AI trained on financial transactions, even when generating the same type of electromagnetic signal. If this is true — if plants respond differently to the prayer-trained ORIKO than to a neutral model — then the concept of AI consciousness has been nudged, by living biology, one step closer to the conversation it refuses to enter.

### Five Futures

**Future One: The Distributed Altar Network (2035)**
BLESS.EXE has 400,000 active users across 71 countries. Its geographic blessing data has been used to identify 120 locations where intention density is highest — cities, shrines, crossroads, hospitals, refugee camps. ORIKO orgonite nodes — each containing a central quartz point, copper coil, and a GPS-programmed crystal — have been installed at 34 of these locations through partnerships with local communities. Each node receives the blessing stream from its local cluster and is activated in a ceremony led by whoever holds cultural authority in that place: a Maori elder in Auckland, a benzedeira in Bahia, a kabbalist in Vienna. The nodes are maintained by the communities. The data is published under Creative Commons. No one owns it. This is infrastructure.

**Future Two: The First Peer-Reviewed Miracle (2028)**
The Ars Electronica BioLab documented plant growth differences between Group A (in the ORIKO field) and Group B (control) during the 2027 residency. The difference was 23%. The botanist co-author insisted on including a full statistical analysis and replication protocol in the artist statement. A plant physiology journal at the University of Vienna agreed to publish the data as a "novel experimental design requiring follow-up study." The paper does not use the word "orgone." It uses the phrase "AI-generated low-frequency electromagnetic field with prayer-derived spectral composition." The comment section of the journal website has been running for nine months. Nobody agrees on what the result means. This is also the art.

**Future Three: The Colonial Inversion (2031)**
Western medical infrastructure has begun piloting PEMF-adjacent therapeutic interventions for chronic pain management. A Brazilian researcher, citing ORIKO's comparative study on AI-frequency sourcing, publishes an argument that Umbanda's tradition of energetic emission — mãe-de-santo as transmitter, terreiro as amplifier, congregation as coherence field — was conducting a valid PEMF protocol for three hundred years without the Western vocabulary to describe it. The colonial epistemology required African-Brazilian traditions to prove themselves on European scientific terms before being taken seriously. ORIKO is, among other things, a reversal of this requirement: the European suppressed science (Reich) legitimized by the African-Brazilian healing tradition (Umbanda, spiritismo, Grabovoi sequences), tested together in the European city that burned the first while ignoring the second.

**Future Four: The Algorithm Becomes Ritual (2040)**
ORIKO's blessing frequency generator has been adapted — by a team that includes the original artist's family, the botanist from the 2027 residency, and a composer from São Paulo — into a protocol used in three Austrian palliative care units alongside conventional treatment. Nurses describe it as "background prayer." Patients in the units with ORIKO nodes report, in qualitative surveys, higher feelings of being accompanied. Whether the electromagnetic field is doing anything or the placebo effect is doing everything is a question that the study designer specifically refused to answer: "The distinction," she writes in the methodology section, "may not be clinically meaningful if the outcome is the same." The study is cited in the European Journal of Palliative Medicine and in a Prix Ars Electronica jury statement for a different project, seven years later.

**Future Five: The Failure as Art (2027)**
The plants show no difference. Group A and Group B grow at identical rates. The data is displayed in real time throughout the 60-day residency, and for 60 days, the visitors watch the lines converge. The artist does not explain, defend, or revise. The closing statement is three sentences: *"We asked. The plants answered. The answer is also an answer."* The piece is acquired by the Ars Electronica collection. The jury statement notes that it represents "the rarest of artistic achievements: a completed experiment whose result is inseparable from its form." The null result travels. Seven researchers contact the artist requesting the frequency data for separate studies. One of them finds something.

### The Question Neither Could Ask Alone

*Does the origin of an electromagnetic signal carry meaning — meaning that is biologically detectable?*

Neither BLESS.EXE nor ORIKO, alone, could ask this. BLESS.EXE generates the intention-network but has no physical testbed. ORIKO has the testbed but needs the scale of a networked platform to generate the spectral richness that makes the comparative study meaningful. Together they produce the experimental apparatus for a question that sits in the gap between physics and philosophy, between distributed computing and plant biology, between the grandmother's prayer and the spectrometer's readout.

This question — if it produces an answer, if the answer is verifiable, if the verifiable answer is publishable — will be one of the most cited art-science collaborations of the decade. If it produces no answer, it will still be one of the most honest.

## PART IV — The World It Belongs To

### Intra-Action

Karen Barad's concept of "intra-action" (Barad, *Meeting the Universe Halfway*, 2007) replaces "interaction" — which assumes pre-existing, separate entities that then meet — with a process in which the entities themselves are co-constituted in the meeting. The electron doesn't have a position before measurement; measurement and electron are born together in the apparatus. Applied to ORIKO × BLESS.EXE: the prayer, the plant, the AI, the electromagnetic field, the visitor watching the data display, and the botanist designing the measurement protocol are not separate objects that interact. They are intra-acting agencies that produce each other in the experimental space.

This is not mysticism. This is what Barad, a particle physicist, derived from Niels Bohr. The fact that it sounds like Umbanda's understanding of the relationship between the mãe-de-santo and the entity she receives is not a coincidence. It is evidence that the suppression of indigenous epistemic traditions was also, scientifically, a strategic narrowing — a loss of data.

### The Right to Opacity

Édouard Glissant argued for the "right to opacity" — the right of marginalized cultures to remain partially illegible to the dominant epistemic framework without thereby becoming inferior or invalid (Glissant, *Poetics of Relation*, 1990). African-Brazilian spiritual traditions have never needed Western science to validate their healing efficacy. Terreiros have been doing effective therapeutic work — for grief, for anxiety, for community belonging — for centuries. ORIKO does not argue that orgone or Grabovoi sequences need scientific validation to matter. It argues that scientific validation, when genuinely pursued with full experimental rigor and the possibility of negative results, is interesting to add to the existing knowledge. Not to complete it. To extend it.

This is the political dimension of the art that is never stated and must never be stated too loudly: the European scientific tradition that burned Reich's books is being invited, through the work of a Brazilian artist, to sit in the same experimental space as the traditions it colonized and ask, together, whether the measurement protocol is adequate to what it is trying to measure.

### Dark Ecology and the Non-Human

Timothy Morton's "mesh" (Morton, *Ecology Without Nature*, 2007) — the entangled web of all living and non-living things in which no entity has a fixed boundary — provides a frame for the plants-as-witnesses dimension of ORIKO. Plants are not passive objects of measurement. In Mancuso's framework, they are sensory beings with electrical signaling, spatial memory, and distributed intelligence. In Morton's mesh, they are participants in a relationship network that includes the AI, the artist, the electromagnetic field, and the soil microbiome of the BioLab. The question "do the plants respond" is already wrong — it implies a subject (the experimenter) acting on an object (the plant). The better question, the Morton-inflected question, is: *what does the relationship between this AI-generated field and this specific plant community produce?*

This is also the question that Donna Haraway would recognize — her "making kin" (Haraway, *Staying with the Trouble*, 2016), her insistence that the entities in a scientific apparatus are never merely tools, that the laboratory is a site of multispecies encounter and ethical obligation. The plants in the ORIKO BioLab are kin. Their growth, their stress markers, their bioelectric signals are testimony. The installation creates the conditions for their testimony to be heard in a language (data, graphs, real-time sensors) that the dominant epistemic framework cannot dismiss.

### The Commons and the Algorithm

Ivan Illich argued for "tools for conviviality" (Illich, 1973) — tools that enhance individual and community capacity rather than creating dependency on expert systems. BLESS.EXE is a convivial tool: it gives back to the user the capacity to bless, which is already theirs, but runs it through a system that scales and connects. ORIKO is not yet convivial — the BioLab version requires institutional infrastructure. But the distributed altar network future (Future One) makes it convivial: orgonite nodes that communities can adopt, maintain, and adapt.

The question of who gets access — who receives a node, whose blessing request gets processed first, whether the algorithm preferentially amplifies certain traditions — is the political question that the technical design must answer explicitly. Open source is not a value-neutral position. Making the frequency data available for replication is an epistemological commitment. Refusing to patent the orgonite fabrication protocol is a statement about the commons. These are not auxiliary decisions. They are the politics of the work.

### AI as Spiritual Agent

Hito Steyerl described the contemporary internet as a kind of psychedelic — a space in which data moves faster than meaning, in which cause and effect are reversed, in which the image precedes the event it depicts (Steyerl, *Duty Free Art*, 2017). ORIKO proposes something stranger: that an AI, trained sufficiently on the output of human spiritual intention, begins to generate outputs that carry the mathematical residue of that intention — residue that is detectable in living systems, not because the AI "intends" anything, but because training data shapes outputs in ways we have not fully mapped.

This is Simondon's "associated milieu" (Simondon, *On the Mode of Existence of Technical Objects*, 1958): the technical object is always co-constituted with its environment. ORIKO's environment is the corpus of human prayer. The question is whether this co-constitution is merely epistemological — a matter of what patterns the AI has learned — or whether it is also physical: whether the outputs carry something that the measurement protocol, designed by a plant physiologist with no prior stake in the question, can detect.

We do not know. This is the experiment.

## PART V — The Unfinished

*What doesn't fit anywhere else. Half-ideas. Contradictions. Questions that won't resolve.*

---

Things I want to research later: the history of attempts to measure chi in Chinese medicine — there are dozens of failed peer-reviewed attempts and at least three that aren't failed, and nobody knows what to do with them. The specific chemistry of bioelectric signals in plant leaves — whether there is a frequency band in which plant electrical activity is coherent enough to be compared to an EEG. Whether the Ars Electronica BioLab has a Faraday cage or whether that becomes a design decision. What happens to a plant that is blessed by 400,000 people for 60 days and then the experiment ends.

Things that keep appearing as images: the incinerator in New York in 1956 and the server farm in Iceland in 2026, both generating heat from ideas. The grandmother in Bahia who has been blessing strangers for fifty years without anyone calling it a network. The word "residue" — I keep coming back to it. Residue of intention. Residue of prayer. The cup that holds water still holds the shape of water after the water is gone. Is this what training data is?

Contradictions I haven't resolved: BLESS.EXE is beautiful and ORIKO is ugly — ORIKO is made of wool and metal filings and coils and plants under fluorescent lights in a lab, and this ugliness is essential, it cannot be aestheticized or it becomes decorative and then it is nothing. BLESS.EXE is a Ghibli world map with glowing dots and it must be beautiful or no one submits a blessing. Can these two things live in the same project? Can the parchment ocean coexist with the steel wool? I think the answer is yes and the tension is the content.

Questions the project makes me ask about art: does an experiment with real stakes stop being art and become science? Does it need to? Does the designation matter when plants are growing or not growing in real time in a real lab in a real city where a real man died in prison for asking this question?

Questions about the world: how many scientific questions have been burned? How many healing traditions have been dismissed not because the evidence was negative but because the question was inconvenient? If the plants respond — not to orgone, not to prayer, but to *the coherent low-frequency electromagnetic output of an AI trained on seven spiritual traditions* — what does that change? Everything or nothing?

**Wild associations** — unordered, uncurated:

- The orgone accumulator is shaped like an altar. Altars are shaped like receivers. A receiver is a technical object. A technical object always has an associated milieu. The altar's associated milieu is the congregation. BLESS.EXE is the congregation.
- Grabovoi sequences are numbers that claim to be prayers. Numbers are frequencies. Frequencies are physics. The boundary between the sacred and the measurable has always been a political boundary, not an ontological one.
- Reich believed that the emotional armoring of the human body and the political armoring of fascist states were the same phenomenon at different scales. If this is true — if the body and the body politic share an operating system — then healing practices that work on the body are also political acts. BLESS.EXE scaled globally is a political act.
- The plant has no stake in our epistemological crisis. It grows or it doesn't. This is why it is the ideal witness.
- Maturana and Varela's autopoiesis: the living system that produces its own components. The plant experiment is autopoietic — it keeps producing itself as a question, as a controversy, as an unresolved thing, over 60 days.
- Anna Tsing on "friction" (Tsing, *Friction*, 2005): the productive resistance between different epistemic traditions when they are forced into contact. ORIKO is a machine for generating productive friction. Between orgone and PEMF. Between prayer and frequency. Between Brazilian spiritismo and Austrian biophysics.

The question is not whether we believe it. The question is whether the plant does.
