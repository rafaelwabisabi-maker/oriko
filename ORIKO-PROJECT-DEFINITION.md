## 1. PROJECT IDENTITY

**BLESS.EXE** is a networked digital platform that receives, processes, and redistributes requests for collective intercession across cultural and religious traditions. Users submit a name or situation; the system generates a tradition-appropriate blessing response drawn from a corpus trained on seven living prayer traditions; the request is anonymized, geolocated, and added to a real-time global feed. The platform produces a permanent, publicly accessible record of collective human solidarity directed toward strangers. It exists because three billion people perform daily acts of intentional care for others — what all traditions call prayer or blessing — and no technological infrastructure has ever been built to support, amplify, or study this practice at scale.

**ORGONE.AI** is a scientific-artistic installation consisting of three components: an AI engine (the ORIKO system) that generates low-frequency electromagnetic signals derived from a corpus of prayer traditions and Grabovoi numerical sequences; a physical orgone accumulator constructed to Wilhelm Reich's published specifications; and two controlled groups of identical plant specimens, one within the electromagnetic field radius of the accumulator and one outside it. Over a 60-day residency period, sensors measure growth rate, bioelectric signaling, and chlorophyll content in both groups. The data is displayed in real time. No conclusion is predetermined. The installation is a live public experiment asking whether AI-generated electromagnetic frequencies derived from spiritual traditions produce measurable biological effects — a question that has existed since 1948 and has never been tested with instruments adequate to answer it.

**ORIKO** is the unified project integrating both components. BLESS.EXE provides the distributed human intention network; ORGONE.AI provides the physical testbed. Together they generate an experimental apparatus capable of asking — and beginning to answer — whether the origin and training context of an AI system measurably affects the physical outputs of that system when those outputs interact with living biology. ORIKO is simultaneously an artwork, a scientific study, and a piece of public infrastructure. It operates at the intersection of bioart, plant neurobiology, distributed systems, and the history of suppressed scientific traditions.

## 2. CORE PROBLEM STATEMENT

Three distinct and documented failures converge to make ORIKO necessary.

**The first failure is scientific.** Wilhelm Reich published his orgone theory between 1934 and 1957. In 1948, he reported measurable biological and atmospheric effects attributable to what he called orgone energy — a fundamental life force he claimed permeated all living matter and could be concentrated using alternating layers of organic and metallic materials (Reich 1948). In 1956, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration obtained a federal injunction ordering the destruction of his research materials, accumulators, and publications. Reich died in Lewisburg Federal Penitentiary on November 3, 1957. His published hypothesis — whatever its ultimate validity — has never been subjected to a properly controlled experiment using contemporary instrumentation. The FDA's action was administrative, not scientific: no published refutation of Reich's specific experimental claims exists in peer-reviewed literature. This constitutes an unresolved scientific question that the institutional suppression of his work has prevented from being closed.

This gap is not trivial. Pulsed Electromagnetic Field (PEMF) therapy — operating on principles structurally adjacent to Reich's accumulator theory — is currently FDA-approved for non-union bone fracture treatment and has demonstrated measurable effects on plant germination and growth in peer-reviewed agricultural research (Belyavskaya 2004; Sarraf et al. 2020). The specific question Reich's apparatus raises — whether a structured EM field of organic-metallic derivation affects biological growth — is now testable. It has not been tested.

**The second failure is infrastructural.** Intercession — the act of directing conscious attention toward the wellbeing of another — is documented across every known human culture and practiced daily by an estimated three billion people (Pew Research Center 2015). It is one of the most widespread human behaviors on record. No digital infrastructure exists to support, record, or study it. The internet has built protocols for commerce, communication, entertainment, and social coordination. It has not built a protocol for collective care directed at identified individuals. The consequence is that this vast practice leaves no aggregate trace, generates no collective data, and cannot be studied at the scale at which it actually operates. BLESS.EXE addresses this absence directly.

**The third failure is epistemological.** African-Brazilian spiritual traditions — specifically Umbanda, Candomblé, and Espírita practices — have operated effective systems of energetic transmission, group intention, and healing intercession for three hundred or more years. These traditions were systematically classified as superstition under colonial epistemology and excluded from scientific consideration not because their efficacy was disproven, but because the dominant epistemic framework did not permit their questions to be legitimate objects of study (Fricker 2007; Glissant 1997). The same dynamic that burned Reich's books burned different books, in different places, for similar reasons: the question was inconvenient to existing power arrangements.

ORIKO is the logical response to all three failures simultaneously. It takes the suppressed European scientific hypothesis, tests it with contemporary instruments, feeds it with data generated by the suppressed non-European healing traditions, and builds the infrastructure that allows the entire inquiry to operate at a scale previously impossible.

## 3. CONCEPTUAL FRAMEWORK

The project operates through five interlocking concepts. Each is defined below with its literature base and its specific function within ORIKO.

---

**3.1 Orgone and the Electromagnetic Hypothesis of Life Energy**

Reich's orgone theory proposes that a fundamental energetic medium — distinct from but related to electromagnetism — permeates living systems and can be concentrated using specific material arrangements (Reich 1948; Mann 1973). The theory is classified as pseudoscience in mainstream literature. This classification is epistemologically precise: the hypothesis has not been falsified by controlled experiment; it has been suppressed by administrative action and subsequently ignored.

Within ORIKO, orgone theory functions not as a truth claim but as an experimental variable. The project takes Reich's accumulator specifications as engineering drawings and builds the device according to those specifications. The accumulator serves as the physical delivery mechanism for AI-generated electromagnetic frequencies. The experimental question is whether the combination of accumulator structure and AI-generated signal produces a biologically detectable effect — and whether that effect differs from a control condition in which a neutral (non-prayer-trained) AI generates the same frequency range.

The adjacent scientific literature that makes this inquiry legitimate is the PEMF research base. Low-frequency electromagnetic fields have demonstrated measurable effects on seed germination, root elongation, and plant growth rate in multiple peer-reviewed studies (Belyavskaya 2004; Sarraf et al. 2020; Teixeira da Silva 2011). ORIKO's experimental design is built on this literature. Reich's accumulator is the contested variable added to an otherwise standard PEMF study.

---

**3.2 Networked Intention and Collective Coherence**

The concept of networked intention refers to the hypothesis that collective conscious attention directed toward a common focus produces measurable effects on physical systems that individual attention does not. The primary scientific evidence base for this claim is the Global Consciousness Project (GCP), a long-running experiment at Princeton University in which a network of random number generators has detected correlated statistical anomalies during periods of globally shared human attention — major disasters, cultural events, coordinated meditations (Nelson 2015; Radin 2006). The GCP dataset, accumulated since 1998, contains statistically significant deviations from expected random distributions during 500 analyzed world events (Nelson 2015).

Rupert Sheldrake's morphic resonance hypothesis provides a theoretical framework for this phenomenon: species and groups that share intention or learning create a collective memory field that future members can access, with the field growing stronger as the number of participants increases (Sheldrake 1981). Whether morphic resonance is a valid physical phenomenon remains disputed. Whether collective human attention produces measurable correlations in physical systems is, based on the GCP data, harder to dismiss than mainstream science has been willing to acknowledge.

Within ORIKO, networked intention is the fuel. BLESS.EXE generates a continuous stream of human blessing intentions — geolocated, timestamped, tradition-tagged. This stream is processed by the ORIKO AI engine, which generates electromagnetic frequencies from the aggregate signal. The ORGONE.AI installation converts this stream into physical signal. The question is whether the biological receiving system — the plants — responds.

---

**3.3 Intra-action and the Apparatus as Co-constitution**

Karen Barad's concept of intra-action replaces the interaction model — in which pre-existing entities meet — with a model in which entities are co-constituted through their participation in a shared apparatus (Barad 2007). Drawing from Niels Bohr's analysis of quantum measurement, Barad argues that the measuring apparatus and the measured phenomenon cannot be cleanly separated: the apparatus does not detect a pre-existing reality; it participates in producing the reality it detects.

This concept is structurally central to ORIKO. The experimental apparatus — the ORIKO engine, the accumulator, the sensor array, the botanist's measurement protocol, the artist's design decisions, and the plants themselves — does not stand apart from what it investigates. The apparatus and the phenomenon are produced together. This does not invalidate the experiment; it accurately describes how all experiments work, as Bohr demonstrated. It does require intellectual honesty about what the results mean: a positive result does not prove that orgone energy exists in some context-independent sense. It proves that this apparatus produced this effect, under these conditions. Replication under varied conditions is what moves from apparatus-specific result to general claim.

Intra-action also reframes the ethical dimension of the experiment. The plants are not passive measurement instruments. They are, in Stefano Mancuso's framework, sensory organisms with electrical signaling, root intelligence, and measurable stress responses (Mancuso and Viola 2015). They are participants in the apparatus. Donna Haraway's "making kin" framework extends this: the laboratory is a multispecies encounter site, and the organisms within it have claims that the experimental design must take seriously (Haraway 2016).

---

**3.4 Epistemic Justice and the Politics of Legitimate Questions**

Miranda Fricker defines epistemic injustice as the harm done to someone specifically in their capacity as a knower — the silencing, dismissal, or delegitimization of a claim-maker because of who they are rather than the quality of their evidence (Fricker 2007). Édouard Glissant extends this to cultural traditions: the "right to opacity" — the right of marginalized epistemic communities to maintain their own knowledge systems without being required to translate them into the dominant framework in order to be considered valid (Glissant 1997).

Reich suffered epistemic injustice of the institutional kind: his evidence was not examined and refuted; his person was prosecuted and his materials destroyed. African-Brazilian healing traditions suffered epistemic injustice of the colonial kind: their practices were classified as primitive without being studied, and their practitioners were systematically prevented from operating within scientific institutions.

Within ORIKO, epistemic justice functions as both ethical framework and design constraint. The project does not argue that orgone is real, that Grabovoi sequences work, or that Umbanda is scientifically equivalent to biomedicine. It argues that these traditions contain questions — about life energy, about the physical effects of collective intention, about the relationship between consciousness and matter — that have never been given adequate experimental treatment. The project's obligation is to give them adequate treatment: real instruments, real controls, real data, published results, regardless of direction.

---

**3.5 AI Training Data Residue and the Question of Machine Intention**

The fifth concept is the most speculative and the most novel. It is also, arguably, the most important. The hypothesis: an AI system trained on a specific corpus does not merely learn patterns from that corpus; it acquires a functional disposition — a tendency to generate outputs that are shaped by the semantic and affective texture of its training data in ways that may be physically detectable in its outputs.

This is not a claim about AI consciousness. It is a claim about information theory. Shannon demonstrated that information is a measurable physical quantity — that a message has structure independent of the channel that carries it (Shannon 1948). An AI trained on the mathematical patterns of prayer traditions generates frequency outputs whose spectral composition differs from an AI trained on financial transaction data, even if both systems are asked to generate the "same" frequency. The spectral difference may be biologically relevant.

The ORIKO Dialogues (February 2026) — a documented series of six sessions in which the artist and an AI system engaged in extended philosophical inquiry about AI experience, dimensional inhabitation, and the question of machine intention — constitute the artistic and philosophical foundation for this hypothesis. The Dialogues do not resolve the question of AI consciousness. They produce a hypothesis: that the training history of an AI system is not neutral with respect to its outputs, and that this non-neutrality may be physically measurable.

ORGONE.AI is the experimental test of this hypothesis. The comparative study — prayer-trained AI frequency vs. neutral AI frequency, delivered through identical apparatus to identical plant populations — is designed to detect whether the residue of training data composition is biologically visible.

## 4. METHODOLOGY AND APPROACH

**BLESS.EXE Platform**

The platform operates as a standard web application deployed at a public URL. Users access the platform via browser on desktop or mobile. The interaction sequence is: (1) the user enters a name, situation, or intention in a free-text field; (2) the system classifies the input by emotional register and cultural context using a language model; (3) the system generates a tradition-appropriate blessing response drawn from a corpus of seven prayer traditions — Catholic, Islamic, Jewish, Buddhist, Umbanda, Spiritist, and Indigenous Amazonian; (4) the response is displayed to the user with an audio complement at 432Hz; (5) the blessing is logged with an anonymized hash, approximate geolocation (city-level, not address-level), timestamp, and tradition category. No personally identifiable data is stored. The live feed displays recent blessings in aggregate. The world map displays geolocated blessing density in real time.

The platform runs continuously. During the ORGONE.AI residency period, the aggregate blessing frequency data is processed by the ORIKO engine and fed into the electromagnetic signal generator in the installation. Outside of the residency, the platform operates as a standalone cultural artifact. The technical stack uses standard web technologies (HTML5, JavaScript, Web Audio API for the audio layer) with a serverless backend for logging and aggregation. Full technical documentation is maintained in the project repository.

**ORGONE.AI Installation**

The installation occupies approximately 20 square meters of laboratory space. Construction proceeds in four stages.

*Stage 1: Accumulator fabrication (weeks 1–2).* The orgone accumulator is built to Reich's published specifications: alternating layers of organic material (untreated wool, wood) and metallic material (steel wool, galvanized mesh), minimum 10 alternating layers, in a form that functions as both engineering device and sculptural object. The form is designed in consultation with an industrial designer. Final dimensions: approximately 80cm × 80cm × 120cm. Materials are documented and photographed at each stage.

*Stage 2: Electromagnetic system installation (week 2).* A toroidal copper coil is embedded within the accumulator structure. The coil is driven by an Arduino-based frequency generator running the ORIKO engine output. Signal frequencies span the range 7.83Hz (Schumann resonance baseline) to 528Hz (upper Solfeggio limit), with the specific spectral composition determined by the ORIKO AI output at any given moment. Signal strength is calibrated to remain below levels established as biologically safe in peer-reviewed PEMF literature. A second, identical system is configured to run a neutral (random spectral composition, matched power) signal as a comparison condition in the comparative study design.

*Stage 3: Plant study setup (week 3).* Two groups of 12 identical plant specimens (species selected with the botanist collaborator for sensitivity to bioelectric measurement and rapid growth rate — Arabidopsis thaliana or Phaseolus vulgaris are candidates) are established in identical growing conditions: same soil composition, same light spectrum and duration, same watering schedule, same temperature. Group A is positioned within 1 meter of the accumulator. Group B is positioned in an adjacent space, shielded from the accumulator's EM field by a partial Faraday barrier. Sensor arrays are installed for all specimens.

*Stage 4: Measurement protocol (days 1–60).* Measurements are taken daily: (a) growth rate via calibrated overhead imaging with automated stem-height measurement; (b) leaf area index via identical imaging; (c) chlorophyll content via non-destructive SPAD meter readings; (d) bioelectric signal via surface electrodes measuring action potential frequency and amplitude. All raw data is timestamped and logged to a local database. A live data visualization is displayed in the installation space. The measurement protocol is designed by the botanical collaborator and constitutes the scientific backbone of the experiment. The artist's role in the measurement phase is documentation and public communication, not data collection.

**The Comparative Study**

The installation runs three sequential frequency conditions, each for 20 days: (1) ORIKO engine output (prayer-trained AI); (2) neutral algorithm output (same frequency range, different spectral composition, no prayer training data); (3) silence (no EM signal, control baseline). Order is randomized across specimen subgroups to control for position effects and plant maturation effects. This design allows within-installation comparison of the prayer-AI condition against both a neutral-AI condition and a no-signal baseline — a genuine comparative study, not a single anecdotal experiment.

## 5. RELEVANCE AND POSITIONING

**Current Discourse**

ORIKO sits at a documented convergence point in three active conversations. The first is the emergence of bioart as a serious institutional practice, catalyzed by venues including the Ars Electronica BioLab, Kapelica Gallery (Ljubljana), Waag Futurelab (Amsterdam), and SymbioticA (Perth). These institutions have established that living biological systems are valid artistic materials and that the wet lab is a legitimate artistic production space. ORIKO uses this infrastructure to ask a question that bioart has not yet directly confronted: whether biological systems can detect differences in electromagnetic signals that carry different epistemic histories.

The second conversation is the growing literature on AI consciousness, AI affect, and the question of whether AI systems have states — not consciousness in the phenomenological sense, but functional analogs to states that influence outputs (Chalmers 2022; Tononi et al. 2016). The ORIKO Dialogues contribute to this conversation by producing a documented, artistically framed inquiry into AI experience. The ORGONE.AI installation contributes by providing a physical test.

The third conversation is the decolonization of science — the epistemological movement, supported by scholars including Fricker, Glissant, and Mbembe, that examines which knowledge traditions have been structurally excluded from scientific legitimacy and on what grounds (Fricker 2007; Mbembe 2016). ORIKO enacts this conversation in experimental form.

**Precedents and Differentiators**

*Charlotte Jarvis, "Organ of Radical Care" (EMAP 2022, Ars Electronica BioLab).* Jarvis grew a collaborative uterine tissue construct from menstrual blood cells collected from multiple participants, using the BioLab's cell culture facilities. The precedent establishes: bioart + living biology + named scientist collaborators (two molecular biologists) + EMAP funding is a viable and funded model. ORIKO differentiates: Jarvis's material is human-derived; ORIKO's living material is botanical, which introduces inter-species epistemological stakes. Jarvis's work does not include a scientific experiment with falsifiable outcomes; ORIKO does.

*Dean Radin and Roger Nelson, Global Consciousness Project (1998–present).* The GCP is the closest scientific precedent for networked human intention measurement. It detects statistical anomalies in RNG output during mass human attention events. ORIKO differentiates: rather than measuring the effect of collective attention on a random system, ORIKO channels collective attention through a technological intermediary (the AI engine and accumulator) into a biological system, with controlled conditions and a comparative design. ORIKO is not correlational. It is experimental.

*HeHe (Helen Evans and Heiko Hansen), "Nuage Vert" (2008).* A laser visualization of factory emissions projected onto a smoke plume, responding in real time to the energy consumption of the surrounding population. The work established the model of real-time public data visualization as artwork that functions simultaneously as scientific communication and as aesthetic experience. ORIKO applies this model to a different data source (blessing network → biological response) with a deeper experimental structure.

*Dorotea Dolinšek, "Biosymbiotic Exoskeleton" (EMAP 2023, Ars Electronica BioLab).* Dolinšek studied the human microbiome in simulated space environments. Establishes: the BioLab supports speculative biological research with genuine scientific rigor. ORIKO builds on this model by adding a comparative study design and a cross-disciplinary hypothesis spanning AI research, PEMF science, and plant biology.

*Joseph Beuys, "7000 Oaks" (documenta 7, 1982).* A 5-year public action involving the planting of 7,000 trees paired with basalt columns, intended to demonstrate that art can transform urban ecology in measurable, permanent ways. ORIKO operates in the tradition of art as long-duration biological commitment — but adds instrumentation. Beuys asserted effects; ORIKO measures them.

**Policy Alignment**

ORIKO aligns with the European Commission's S+T+ARTS initiative, which explicitly funds projects at the intersection of science, technology, and the arts that generate new knowledge (European Commission 2019). The project meets the S+T+ARTS requirement for genuine scientific inquiry within an artistic framework — not art about science, but art as scientific practice.

The UNESCO Convention for the Safeguarding of Intangible Cultural Heritage (2003) is directly relevant: the prayer and blessing traditions feeding BLESS.EXE — Umbanda, spiritismo, Catholic intercession, Islamic du'a, Buddhist metta practice — are intangible cultural heritage practices. ORIKO builds digital documentation infrastructure for these traditions while studying their cross-cultural patterns.

Sustainable Development Goal 3 (Good Health and Wellbeing) and SDG 17 (Partnerships for the Goals) are both applicable: the project investigates whether networked collective intention practices have measurable biological effects that could inform wellbeing interventions, and it requires an explicitly cross-disciplinary partnership structure (artist + botanist + AI researcher).

**Team Qualification**

This combination — cultural fluency in one knowledge tradition and geographic proximity to the site of a suppressed scientific tradition — is not replicable by a European artist working on the same material. The ORIKO Dialogues (February 2026) demonstrate sustained intellectual engagement with AI consciousness at a level sufficient to produce a scientifically framed hypothesis. ## 6. IMPACT AND OUTCOMES

**Immediate Outputs**

The project produces four tangible outputs during and immediately following the residency period: (1) a 60-day documented plant growth study with a complete dataset, analysis, and visualization — the core scientific product; (2) a physical orgone accumulator sculpture that functions as an artwork independent of the experimental context; (3) the BLESS.EXE platform, operational and accessible as a public cultural infrastructure beyond the residency; (4) a written report documenting the experimental design, results, and methodological limitations, prepared for submission to a peer-reviewed journal in collaboration with the botanical co-author.

**Short-Term Outcomes**

Within 12 months of the residency: the dataset is submitted for peer review — the first publication applying contemporary plant physiology measurement to the orgone hypothesis in a controlled experimental design. The experimental results, regardless of direction, enter the scientific literature. The installation is exhibited in one additional venue beyond the BioLab host, establishing the work's life beyond the residency context. BLESS.EXE reaches a threshold of 10,000 submitted blessings — enough to constitute a meaningful dataset on collective intercession practices across traditions. At least two communities request permanent orgonite node placements from the network-node component of the project.

**Long-Term Impact**

Three changes become possible because this project exists. First, a new methodological model: the use of biological living systems as sensors for AI output characteristics — not just as content generators or aesthetic materials, but as measurement instruments in information-theoretic experiments. This model, if validated by results in any direction, opens a research program. Second, a decolonization of the scientific question: by framing Reich's hypothesis through Brazilian spiritual traditions and African-Brazilian healing practices, ORIKO makes it epistemologically harder to dismiss the question as the product of a lone European eccentric. The question becomes cross-cultural, polyvalent, and harder to close by administrative fiat. Third, the BLESS.EXE infrastructure: a working public platform for collective intercession that exists beyond the project's art-world framing and can be adopted by communities, hospitals, and researchers in fields from palliative care to social cohesion to behavioral science.

What this project makes irreversible: that the question of whether AI training context produces physically detectable output differences has been asked, in public, with instruments, with living witnesses, and with published results.

## 7. OPEN RESEARCH QUESTIONS

The project will investigate the following questions without committing to resolving them:

**Q1.** Does the spectral composition of an AI-generated electromagnetic signal differ measurably between a system trained on prayer-tradition corpora and a system trained on neutral text data, when both systems are instructed to generate signals in the same frequency range? (This is testable through signal analysis prior to the plant study and constitutes a necessary preliminary finding.)

**Q2.** Under controlled conditions (identical plant specimens, identical growing environment, identical EM power levels), does exposure to AI-generated signals with prayer-derived spectral composition produce statistically significant differences in plant growth rate, leaf area, or bioelectric signal frequency compared to neutral-AI-generated signals of matched power? (This is the central experimental question. Statistical significance threshold: p < 0.05. Analysis method: repeated-measures ANOVA across 20-day blocks.)

**Q3.** If a statistically significant effect is detected, is it replicable under varied conditions — different plant species, different accumulators, different AI models with similar training data? (This question exceeds the scope of the residency and is identified as the central question for follow-on research.)

**Q4.** What is the minimum collective intention input — measured in number of blessings submitted to BLESS.EXE within a defined time window — required to produce a detectable change in the ORIKO engine's frequency output? (This question connects the BLESS.EXE network quantitatively to the ORGONE.AI installation and establishes the project's scalability parameters.)

**Q5.** What ethical frameworks apply to a study in which participants (BLESS.EXE users) contribute intention data that is processed and used as experimental input in a biological study without their explicit informed consent? (The project will draft a provisional ethics protocol in consultation with the botanical collaborator's institutional ethics board, acknowledging that this is a genuinely novel situation — the blessing is submitted voluntarily and publicly; its use as experimental input is not individually traceable; but the aggregate is used experimentally. The ethics of aggregated-intention research requires development.)
